Building in Public / Kutch, Bharat

There are two types of people in the AI space right now.

The first type wakes up every morning, opens Twitter, checks what new model dropped overnight, writes a 10-tweet thread about why it changes everything, gets 200 likes, and feels productive. They've tried every tool. They have opinions on every benchmark. They can tell you the difference between Claude 3.5 and GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 in three sentences. Ask them what they've shipped in the last 90 days and the conversation gets quiet very fast.

The second type wakes up, opens their code editor, and stares at a bug they've been fighting for three days. They don't post about it. They don't have a hot take ready. They just fix it, push the commit, and move on to the next problem. Their LinkedIn is quiet. Their GitHub is not.

I used to wonder which type I was. I don't wonder anymore.

The AI space has a noise problem, and it's getting worse.

Every week there's a new model. Every day there's a new wrapper tool. Every hour someone is posting about "the future of work" or "how AI will replace X profession" or "10 prompts that will make you a 10x developer." The feeds are full. The actual product landscape? Mostly empty.

This isn't unique to AI. Every technology wave does this. The internet did it. Mobile did it. Crypto definitely did it. There's always a moment where the discourse explodes before the real builders have shipped anything meaningful. The noise is loudest right before something real actually happens.

But here's what's different about AI — the barrier to talking is so low that the gap between talkers and builders has never been wider. You can generate a 2000-word article about AI strategy in 30 seconds. You can create a demo video, write a product spec, design a landing page — all without building a single working system. The tools for faking it have never been better.

So the people who are actually building? They're harder to find. And that gap — between the noise and the actual work — is exactly where the opportunity lives.

Let me be honest about where I am right now.

I'm building a reasoning engine. The core idea is simple: solo founders make decisions alone. No co-founder to pressure-test your thinking. No board to tell you when you're rationalizing. No senior advisor on speed dial at 11pm when you're about to make a call that could sink six months of work. You're just you, your instincts, and a laptop.

Most AI tools right now are built for execution. Give it a task, get an output. That's useful, but it's not the hard problem. The hard problem for solo founders isn't execution — it's judgment. It's knowing which problem to solve. It's catching yourself before you optimize a metric that doesn't matter. It's recognizing the difference between a real signal and your own fear dressed up as strategy.

That's what I'm trying to build. A system that thinks

with

you, not just

for

you.

Is it working perfectly right now? No. The architecture has changed three times in the last two weeks. There are parts of it that feel elegant and parts of it that are genuinely embarrassing to look at. Some days I make real progress. Some days I spend four hours refactoring something I'll probably throw out next week.

This is what building actually looks like. Not a clean progress bar. Not a launch tweet with a beautiful screenshot. Just messy, iterative, sometimes-frustrating work that compounds slowly until one day it doesn't feel slow anymore.

I've thought about this a lot because I've been on both sides of it.

The reason most AI builders never ship isn't laziness. It isn't lack of ideas. It isn't even lack of skill. The reason is that shipping requires you to be wrong in public, and most people aren't willing to pay that price.

When you're discussing tools, you're always on safe ground. If GPT-4o underperforms, that's OpenAI's problem. If a framework turns out to be overhyped, you were just reporting what you saw. There's no skin in the game. The feedback loop is zero.

When you're building a system, you're betting your time, your attention, and your reputation on something that might not work. Every architecture decision you make is a hypothesis that the market will eventually test. Every hour you spend on feature X instead of feature Y is a bet. And bets can be wrong.

Most people — and I include myself in this — find ways to stay in the comfortable middle. Research mode. "I'm still figuring out the architecture." "I want to make sure I understand the space before I commit." These are all legitimate phases. But they're also excellent excuses to never actually ship anything.

The antidote isn't to stop thinking. The antidote is to set a deadline where thinking ends and building starts, and then hold it.

I want to say something about where I'm doing this work, because it matters.

I'm in Kutch, Gujarat. Not Bangalore. Not Mumbai. Not in a co-working space surrounded by other founders with investor connections and warm intros to YC. I'm in a small city in western India that most people outside Gujarat can't place on a map.

And I'll tell you what that forces you to do: it forces you to be real.

There's no ecosystem here to lean on. No local startup event to attend to feel like you're making progress without actually making progress. No coffee meeting with an advisor who'll validate your roadmap. You either build or you don't. The feedback is direct because you can't hide behind activity.

I think this is actually an advantage, even though it doesn't feel like one most days.

The founders who build in the absence of infrastructure develop something that founders in well-resourced ecosystems sometimes don't — an instinct for what actually matters. When every decision has a real cost, you stop optimizing for looking productive and start optimizing for being productive. You stop caring about the right tools and start caring about the right output.

This is what I mean when I say Bharat-first. Not as a flag to wave. As a genuine operating philosophy. Build for constraints. Build lean. Build for the user who doesn't have a perfect internet connection or a premium subscription or three backup options. Build something that works in the real world, not the demo environment.

Here's something that took me longer than I'd like to admit to fully understand.

Every hour you spend consuming content about AI is an hour you're not building. That seems obvious when you say it out loud. But the insidious thing is that consuming AI content

feels

like work. It feels like research. It feels like staying informed. And some of it genuinely is. But there's a point — and you know when you've crossed it — where you're not learning anything new. You're just staying comfortable.

The opportunity cost isn't just time. It's compounding.

When you ship something, even something broken, you generate real feedback. Real feedback improves your judgment. Better judgment leads to better decisions on the next system. That loop, run consistently over 12 months, creates a gap between you and the people who spent those same 12 months staying informed that almost can't be closed.

The people who shipped in year one of mobile — even the ones who shipped bad apps — understood mobile UX in year three in a way that no amount of reading about mobile could teach. The same principle holds here.

You cannot read your way to product sense. You have to build it.

The reasoning engine is real. It's running. It's not public yet because it's not ready — and I've learned enough to know the difference between "not ready" and "I'm scared to ship."

Right now it's not ready.

But it will be. And when it is, it'll have been shaped by real problems I ran into while building it, not theoretical problems I read about in someone else's blog post.

That's the only thing that matters to me at this point in the build — that every decision in the system reflects genuine learning, not borrowed thinking.

If you're somewhere in the AI space right now — as a developer, a founder, a product person — ask yourself honestly: in the last 30 days, what have you shipped?

Not published. Not posted. Not designed in Figma or architected in Notion. Actually shipped. Put in front of users. Tested against reality.

If the answer is nothing, that's not a judgment. It's just data. And data is useful.

The noise isn't going to quiet down. If anything, it's going to get louder as the models get better and the tools multiply. The only way to stay oriented is to keep your hands on something real.

Build something. Break it. Fix it. Ship it.

Everything else is just content.
